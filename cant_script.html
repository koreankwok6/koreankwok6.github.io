Introduction

Since the beginning of the Yue people's history, we have been using Chinese characters (漢字). After the Qin Empire conquered Baakjyut (百越 / Baiyue), the Han people has tried to sinicise the indigenous peoples.

As the sense of Hong Kong identity becomes apparent, many has been contributing to Cantonese conservation. Some devote themselves to finding Chinese characters in historical dictionaries, some collect vocabulary items and compile a Cantonese dictionary, and some attempt to allow non-native residents and foreigners to learn Cantonese more easily. The most well-known romanisation is the Jyutping (粵拼) invented by the Linguistics Society of Hong Kong (LSHK). Jyutping aims to notate Cantonese pronunciations with alphanumeric characters without special symbols, making it easy to learn and utilize. However, Jyutping does not convey much meaningful information other than the pronunciations of the Chinese characters, and it does not appear harmonious with the Han characters. Therefore, the Cantonese Script Reform Society (CSRS) proposed a new script inspired by the Chinese characters.

The 'most scientific' script: Hangul

Instead of stacking the scripts left to right and top to bottom, we might want to learn from how the Korean King Sejong invented Hangul for their language, which is named the most scientific writing system around the world. In his Hunminjeong'eum (訓民正音, lit. 'Teaching Peasants the Correct Sounds'), King Sejong explained why he would like to create Hangul.
